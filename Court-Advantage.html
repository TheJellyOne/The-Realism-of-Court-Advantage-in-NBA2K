Introduction

In the NBA2K games, a basketball simulator, the developers go out of their way to make game play and visuals as close to real life basketball as possible. Not only are the character models painstakingly designed to look like their real life counterparts, but signature moves, shot animations, and player attributes for every individual player end up requiring gigabytes of storage space. How all this information is determined in the game is fairly straightforward, being based off of historical data. But there is one tiny attribute that Im not fully sure I understand, and that is Court Advantage for teams. Every court is given a letter grade, and that letter grade impacts the game play of both the home and away teams, whether it be shaking the screen during free throws, or increasing shot percentages in late game scenarios. Historically, studies have shown that the home teams do perform better, and many theories have emerged, ranging from additional pressure on referees to make calls in favor of the home team, players not having to travel and play in foreign conditions, and fans cheering to raise team morale. For now, well focus on how realistic it is for home court advantage to dramatically reduce free throw rates of the away team. This is a completely unbiased point of interest, and has nothing to do with my personal vendetta from missing every free throw as the away team in NBA2K.

The Data

The data used is sourced directly from the NBA api, making it both reliable and extremely easy to clean. I downloaded this dataset from kaggle.com, and used only game data from the last 30 years to avoid team re-brands and re-locations.

First, I need to load all the packages Im using.

library(gghighlight)
library(plotly)
library(tidyverse)
library(RSQLite)

Then to load the sqlite data and create a data frame from it.

con <- dbConnect(drv=SQLite(), dbname="basketball.sqlite")
tables <- dbListTables(con)
tables <- tables[tables != "sqlite_sequence"]
lDataFrames <- vector("list", length=length(tables))

## create a data.frame for each table
for (i in seq(along=tables)) {
  lDataFrames[[i]] <- dbGetQuery(conn=con, statement=paste("SELECT * FROM '", tables[[i]], "'", sep=""))
}

## isolate columns of specific data set
games <- lDataFrames[[3]]
games <- games[ , c("TEAM_ABBREVIATION_HOME", "TEAM_ABBREVIATION_AWAY", "SEASON_ID", "FT_PCT_HOME", "FT_PCT_AWAY")]

I filtered the data set according to what I specified above. The SEASON_ID is the year with a 2 in the front. So to create and initial filter for 1980 and later, I formatted the column as an integer, and then filtered out numbers lower that 21979.

games <- games %>%
  mutate(SEASON_ID = as.integer(SEASON_ID)) %>%
  filter(SEASON_ID > 21979)

Instead, I should be comparing the courts impact to the away teams free throw performance.

Graph #3: Away Team % difference by home court

Lucky attempt number three. For this one, I can disregard the home teams free throw percentage, and only check how the away team does compared to their average. Since I wont distinguish by season this time, Ill filter my data even more to only include only the last few years.

First, filter all the data, and calculate averages for each team.

### filter last decade, group by match ups, calculate average FT%
games_2010 <- filter(games, SEASON_ID>22009)

games_by_away <- games_2010 %>%
  group_by(TEAM_ABBREVIATION_HOME, TEAM_ABBREVIATION_AWAY) %>%
  summarise(AVG_FT_PCT_AWAY = mean(FT_PCT_AWAY))

### filter last decade, group by away teams's average FT%
team_avg_at_home <- games_2010 %>%
  group_by(TEAM_ABBR = TEAM_ABBREVIATION_HOME) %>%
  summarise(TEAM_AVG_HOME = mean(FT_PCT_HOME))

team_avg_away <- games_2010 %>%
  group_by(TEAM_ABBR = TEAM_ABBREVIATION_AWAY) %>%
  summarise(TEAM_AVG_AWAY = mean(FT_PCT_AWAY))

team_avg <- merge(team_avg_at_home, team_avg_away, by="TEAM_ABBR", all.x = TRUE)
team_avg$TEAM_ABBREVIATION_AWAY <- team_avg$TEAM_ABBR
team_avg$TEAM_AVG <- with(team_avg, (TEAM_AVG_HOME + TEAM_AVG_AWAY)/2 )


### calculate difference in average performance.
ft_diff_by_court <- merge(games_by_away, team_avg, by="TEAM_ABBREVIATION_AWAY", all.x = TRUE)
ft_diff_by_court$FT_DIFF <- with(ft_diff_by_court, AVG_FT_PCT_AWAY - TEAM_AVG)
ft_diff_agg <- ft_diff_by_court %>%
  group_by(TEAM_ABBREVIATION_HOME) %>%
  summarise(AVG_DIFF = mean(FT_DIFF))

Although, unsurprising, the team with the largest fan base, the LA Lakers (LAL), is where away teams perform their worst.

Conclusions

My analysis was supposed to include a comparison between my results and the home court advantage found in-game. However, I realized that the ratings in-game change based on which two teams are competing, and also vary depending on the teams current record. So without definitive data to compare to, Ill stop here. The last graph stands on its own, showing that a crowds jeering has very little correlation to the Away Teams free throw rates. Even at its very best, with an average of 22 free throw attempts per team, over a 41 game season (82 game total, half played at home), that -1.5% FT rate for the LA Lakers results in a total of 13 points over a season. Once in a blue moon, a crowds booing might change the outcome of a game, but we should just as well respect that these athletes hardly drop in free throw percentage in their away games, even with the pressure of the crowd against them.
